Optimal Convex Hulls for Low-Rank Data in High-Dimensional Space

A 35-year-old algorithmic lemma turns out to be conditional independence on a DAG. Here is what that buys you — and what it doesn’t.

TL;DR — Scope, stated up front

For data with intrinsic rank r << d, CT-QuickHull computes the exact convex hull in O(nrd) — matching the unconditional lower bound Ω(nrd). That is optimal in this regime.

For full-rank or adversarial high-dimensional data, the algorithm matches the asymptotic complexity of existing methods rather than improving over them. The structural contribution — recognizing the Heritage Lemma as d-separation on an event DAG — is universal and independent of data rank.

§ 1 — The problem

Convex hulls are tractable in low dimensions. In high d, they are not — without structure.

A convex hull is the smallest convex shape that encloses a set of points. In two dimensions, it is the rubber band you would stretch around a scatter of pins. In ten dimensions, fifty, a hundred — the same idea, just much harder to compute. And in the worst case, much harder than “hard”: actually intractable.

The barrier is the Upper Bound Theorem, proved by McMullen in 1970. A polytope with n vertices in d-dimensional space can have up to Θ(n⌊d/2⌋) faces. This is not a worst-case conjecture — it is tight, achieved by specific constructions called cyclic polytopes. Algorithms that examine each face per update step incur cost exponential in d, and they have no way around it.

Concretely: at n = d = 100, the worst-case face count is around 10100. No conceivable hardware addresses that. The exact convex hull problem in high dimensions has sat in the “intractable” pile for half a century.

Most write-ups of this story stop here, then introduce a method, then claim some kind of breakthrough. The honest version requires one more step before the breakthrough: what kind of data are we actually computing hulls of?

Real-world high-dimensional datasets — single-cell genomics readouts, NLP embeddings, sensor arrays, image features — almost never have full rank in their ambient space. The intrinsic dimension of the data, the r in r-dimensional affine subspace where the points actually live, is typically far smaller than the ambient d. The right question is not

“Can we compute exact convex hulls in high d?”

but rather

“Can we compute exact convex hulls in high d when the data has low-rank affine structure?”

That second question is the one CT-QuickHull answers. The first one, in its full generality, it does not. Holding that distinction firmly in mind is the difference between an honest result and an overstated one.

§ 2 — The structural insight

The Heritage Lemma (1989) is a d-separation statement on an event DAG.

In 1989, Clarkson and Shor published a paper on randomised geometric algorithms with a lemma that has been sitting in the computational geometry literature for thirty-five years. Paraphrased:

When a new hull facet τ is created, only points already conflicting with τ’s parents in the deletion history need to be checked. All other points are irrelevant.

Clarkson and Shor used this to bound expected running times in randomised incremental construction. It is a useful algorithmic fact. What we noticed — and what makes the rest of this work possible — is that it is also something else.

In the language of probabilistic graphical models, the Heritage Lemma is a conditional independence statement on a directed acyclic graph. The deletion history of the hull is a DAG. A new facet τ is conditionally independent of all points outside its parents’ conflict sets, given the parents themselves. That is the definition of d-separation. The Heritage Lemma is the certificate of conditional independence at each facet-creation event in the DAG.

If you have worked with Bayesian networks, sparse linear algebra, or message-passing algorithms, you have seen this pattern before. The same structure appears across multiple fields:

  • In belief propagation on Bayesian networks, messages flow only along graph edges to causally connected nodes. Nodes d-separated from an observation receive no message and need no update.
  • In sparse Gaussian elimination, eliminating a pivot updates only rows and columns that share a non-zero with it. Zero entries are d-separated from the pivot in the elimination tree, and no computation touches them.
  • In kinetic data structures, certificate updates after a geometric change are restricted to certificates involving the changed element — the rest are guaranteed unaffected.

The Heritage Lemma is the convex hull instance of this same principle. The Conflict Tree (CT) — the ancestry DAG of the hull construction, with leaves as current facets and edges as replacement history — is the graphical model. Every CT.update operation is one message-passing step on it.

None of these connections required new mathematics. They required noticing that the Heritage Lemma, the elimination tree, and the d-separation property in graphical models are all describing the same underlying structure from different angles.

§ 3 — The mechanism

Lemma 3: per-step cost severed from the global facet count.

Once you see the Heritage Lemma as d-separation, the algorithm design becomes mechanical. CT-QuickHull pairs QuickHull’s farthest-point selection — which, by a Rockafellar-style separation argument, guarantees every selected point is a true hull vertex — with the CT for hull updates. QuickHull picks the next vertex; the CT updates the hull using only Heritage-certified conflict candidates, never touching points outside the parents’ conflict sets.

The resulting per-step cost is the technical heart of the paper:

Lemma 3 — Facet Count Independence Cost of CT.update(v) = O ( |Cvis| · |∂Vis(v)| · d ) The global current-facet count |Ft| does not appear.

The Upper Bound Theorem governs |Ft| globally — that exponential factor is real, and no bookkeeping trick removes it from the total face count of the hull. What Lemma 3 does is sever it from the per-step cost. The work performed at each insertion depends on the local conflict sets and the horizon size, not on how many facets the current hull happens to have.

Universal contribution

Lemma 3 holds for any data, regardless of intrinsic rank. The structural improvement is real on full-rank data, on adversarial geometry, on noisy data, on anything. Whenever pruning reduces |Et|, CT-QuickHull’s selection cost is strictly less than that of conflict-graph randomised incremental construction.

But Lemma 3 alone does not yield the headline complexity result. It removes |Ft| from the per-step cost, not from the total. On data without low-rank structure, Ftotal — the total number of facets ever created over the construction’s lifetime — can still be exponential in d, and the asymptotic order of the algorithm matches existing methods.

The tractability story requires more, and that more is a property of the data, not of the algorithm.

§ 4 — The rank-r result

Theorem 2: For data with intrinsic rank r << d, CT-QuickHull is optimal.

For data lying in an r-dimensional affine subspace embedded in ℝd, three facts from Carathéodory’s theorem combine to give a tight result. Each is an O(1) bound for fixed r:

  • Step count. The hull has at most r + 1 vertices.
  • Hull faces. |Ft| = O(1) for fixed r.
  • Horizon size. |∂Vis(vt)| = O(1), bounded by |Ft|.

These three O(1) facts collapse the output-sensitive formula of Theorem 1 to:

Theorem 2 — Primary Result O(nrd) = Ω(nrd) Tight: matches the unconditional lower bound for any deterministic exact hull algorithm on rank-r data.

What the rank-r assumption is doing: it asserts that the data lives in a low-dimensional affine subspace embedded in the high-dimensional ambient space. This is a real assumption about the data, not a property of the algorithm. When it holds — and for many natural high-dimensional datasets it does — CT-QuickHull achieves the unconditional lower bound. When it does not, the algorithm still runs, still produces the exact hull, but its asymptotic complexity is no better than alternatives.

Honest framing: this is a tractability result for low-rank-structured high-dimensional data, not a tractability result for high-dimensional data in general.

§ 5 — Empirical validation

All five theoretical predictions confirmed on synthetic rank-r data.

We built the algorithm, ran it on synthetic rank-r data with known ground truth, and verified every prediction the theory makes. The primary experiment: n = 100 points in ℝ100, lying in a 10-dimensional affine subspace, with 11 planted simplex vertices known by construction.

Primary experiment: n = 100, d = 100, r = 10
Prediction Observed
Step count h = r + 1 = 1111 ✓

The most striking phenomenon is mass pruning: the 89 interior points are not eliminated gradually across the construction. They are pruned simultaneously, at the final step, when the hull spans the full 10-dimensional subspace. Before then, interior points have positive residual from the partial affine hull and remain in the exterior set. At step 11, the affine span covers all of ℝr and every interior point — at the same instant — has zero residual and is removed.

This is not an artefact of the implementation. It is a geometric property of rank-r data, and it is the operational mechanism behind Corollary 3 (Pruning Amplification) which formalises when CT-QuickHull’s improvement over RIC is large.

The r-scaling experiment confirms the complexity prediction holds across a range of intrinsic dimensions:

The ratio scales linearly with r — an O(r) hidden constant in the leading term, consistent with theory. Selection alone tracks O(nrd) at 1.04× uniformly; the O(r) overhead comes from CT.update at the final step where |∂Vis| reaches r. Every correctness check passes.

§ 6 — What is not claimed

Scope honesty: full-rank and adversarial geometry remain hard.

I want to be precise about the limits of the result, because the contribution is more credible when its scope is stated than when it is implied. Section 9.1 of the paper is explicit on these points; the blog post should be too.

From § 9.1 of the paper

Adversarial geometry (cyclic polytopes). On cyclic polytopes in ℝd, the horizon size |∂Vis(vt)| can be Θ(|Vis|(d-2)/(d-1)), which becomes large. Total CT cost is O(d · n · Ftotal) ≤ O(n1+⌊d/2⌋ · d). Asymptotic order matches RIC; CT improves only by the per-step factor |Et|/n and the step-count factor h/n.

Spherical shell / general position. For n points on the unit sphere in general position with no d+1 in a common hyperplane, every point is a hull vertex (h = n) and |Et| stays roughly n throughout. Selection alone is O(n2d); CT.update adds O(d · n · Ftotal). For d ≥ 4, no asymptotic improvement over RIC.

Comparison scope. Theorems 3 and 4 compare against conflict-graph RIC. Heritage-recursive QuickHull implementations already use conflict locality; CT-QuickHull’s advantage over those is the h/n step-count reduction from guaranteed-vertex selection — not an asymptotic regime change.

None of this diminishes the rank-r result. It locates the result correctly: as a tractability claim for data with low-rank affine structure, with the rest of the high-dimensional convex hull problem still unresolved by this work.

§ 7 — The exportable paradigm

Causal Sparsity System: a framework beyond convex hulls.

The mechanism here — replacing a global combinatorial update with a message-passing step on a causal ancestry DAG, justified by a conditional-independence property at the event boundary — is not specific to convex hull construction. We formalise it as a Causal Sparsity System (CSS), a tuple (Σ, 𝔼, G, ℍ) with:

  • Σ: a state space — combinatorial objects with associated conflict sets.
  • 𝔼: an event sequence — each event deletes some objects and creates new ones.
  • G: an event DAG — recording the ancestry of object creation.
  • ℍ: a Heritage condition — the conditional independence certificate at event boundaries.

The CSS complexity formula is:

Theorem 0 — CSS Complexity T = O ( ctest · Σt |St| · δt ) Proved for the convex hull instance via Lemmas 3 and 5. Conjectural in full generality.

This replaces the naive O(ctest · n · Ftotal) with a formula that scales by causal connectivity rather than global structure size. CT-QuickHull is one CSS instance. We conjecture — and this is open work — that the same paradigm applies to several other domains where an analogous Heritage-type condition is present:

  • Delaunay triangulation. The cavity of violated triangles when a new point is inserted plays the role of Vis(v); the Heritage condition is that points outside the circumspheres of cavity boundary triangles are irrelevant to the new triangles. Conjectured: O(ndr) for rank-r point sets.
  • Hyperplane arrangements. The Zone Theorem bounds the cells intersected by a new hyperplane; zone = Vis(v); Zone Theorem = Heritage condition. The CSS update would restrict to zone ancestry.
  • Sparse matrix refactorisation. The elimination tree records which columns require update after a rank-1 modification. Columns outside the elimination subtree of the modified entry need no update. The elimination tree is the CT for linear algebra; fill-in sparsity is the Heritage Lemma.

These are conjectures, not claims. But the structural parallel is precise enough that proving the general CSS theorem — showing that T = O(ctest · Σ |St| · δt) holds for all CSS instances satisfying Definition 1 — is a well-defined and reachable open problem. It is the primary open problem this work creates.

§ 8 — The contribution, by scope

What is universal, what is rank-r specific, what is conjectural.

Reduced to its components, the contribution decomposes into three categories. They have different evidentiary status and should be cited differently.

Universal

Holds for any data

  • Heritage Lemma reframed as d-separation on event DAG
  • CT is a graphical model; CT.update is one message-passing step
  • Lemma 3: per-step cost independent of |Ft|
  • Strict improvement over RIC whenever pruning occurs
Scoped

Requires rank r << d

  • Theorem 2: O(nrd), matching Ω(nrd)
  • Three Carathéodory bounds collapse the output-sensitive formula
  • Mass pruning at the final step (ρ = 0)
  • Confirmed for r ∈ {2, 5, 10, 15, 20}
Conjectural

Open problems

  • Theorem 0 in full generality (CSS general theorem)
  • Delaunay triangulation as CSS instance
  • Hyperplane arrangements as CSS instance
  • Sparse matrix refactorisation as CSS instance

Citation hygiene matters here. The universal claims can be cited as established. The scoped claims should be cited with their assumption (low-rank affine structure). The conjectural claims should be cited as open problems, not as results.

§ 9 — A personal note

Working at the intersection of fields.

The insight that the Heritage Lemma is a conditional independence statement came from looking at the same structure in three different fields — sparse matrix factorisation, belief propagation, and convex hull construction — and noticing that the same proof sketch worked in all three.

The computational geometry community has known the Heritage Lemma for thirty-five years. The machine learning community has known d-separation on DAGs for almost as long. The numerical linear algebra community has worked with elimination trees and sparse fill-in for decades. Nobody connected them because the fields do not usually talk to each other, and because each community has its own vocabulary for what is structurally the same idea.

That is the thing about working at the intersection of domains: the interesting results tend to live in the gap between communities, not inside any one of them. The CSS framework is an attempt to name the gap, so that the next person who recognises this pattern in their own field can cite it directly rather than rediscovering it under another name.

I would like to hear from anyone working on Delaunay triangulation, sparse linear algebra, hyperplane arrangements, or kinetic data structures who sees the CSS pattern in their own work. The general theorem is out there. It is just waiting for someone to prove it.
